<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:72px; top:271px; width:218px; height:513px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">We report on a series of experiments with
<br>convolutional neural networks (CNN)
<br>trained on top of pre-trained word vec-
<br>tors for sentence-level classiﬁcation tasks.
<br>We show that a simple CNN with lit-
<br>tle hyperparameter tuning and static vec-
<br>tors achieves excellent results on multi-
<br>ple benchmarks. Learning task-speciﬁc
<br>vectors through ﬁne-tuning offers further
<br>gains in performance. We additionally
<br>propose a simple modiﬁcation to the ar-
<br>chitecture to allow for the use of both
<br>task-speciﬁc and static vectors. The CNN
<br>models discussed herein improve upon the
<br>state of the art on 4 out of 7 tasks, which
<br>include sentiment analysis and question
<br>classiﬁcation.
<br></span><span style="font-family: AFMCTD+NimbusRomNo9L-Medi; font-size:15px">1 Introduction
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">Deep learning models have achieved remarkable
<br>results in computer vision (Krizhevsky et al.,
<br>2012) and speech recognition (Graves et al., 2013)
<br>in recent years. Within natural language process-
<br>ing, much of the work with deep learning meth-
<br>ods has involved learning word vector representa-
<br>tions through neural language models (Bengio et
<br>al., 2003; Yih et al., 2011; Mikolov et al., 2013)
<br>and performing composition over the learned word
<br>vectors for classiﬁcation (Collobert et al., 2011).
<br>Word vectors, wherein words are projected from a
<br>sparse, 1-of-</span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">V </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">encoding (here </span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">V </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">is the vocabulary
<br>size) onto a lower dimensional vector space via a
<br>hidden layer, are essentially feature extractors that
<br>encode semantic features of words in their dimen-
<br>sions. In such dense representations, semantically
<br>close words are likewise close—in euclidean or
<br>cosine distance—in the lower dimensional vector
<br>space.
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:72px; top:786px; width:218px; height:26px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">Convolutional neural networks (CNN) utilize
<br>layers with convolving ﬁlters that are applied to
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:307px; top:252px; width:218px; height:107px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">local features (LeCun et al., 1998). Originally
<br>invented for computer vision, CNN models have
<br>subsequently been shown to be effective for NLP
<br>and have achieved excellent results in semantic
<br>parsing (Yih et al., 2014), search query retrieval
<br>(Shen et al., 2014), sentence modeling (Kalch-
<br>brenner et al., 2014), and other traditional NLP
<br>tasks (Collobert et al., 2011).
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:307px; top:361px; width:218px; height:325px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">In the present work, we train a simple CNN with
<br>one layer of convolution on top of word vectors
<br>obtained from an unsupervised neural language
<br>model. These vectors were trained by Mikolov et
<br>al. (2013) on 100 billion words of Google News,
<br>and are publicly available.</span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:9px">1 </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">We initially keep the
<br>word vectors static and learn only the other param-
<br>eters of the model. Despite little tuning of hyper-
<br>parameters, this simple model achieves excellent
<br>results on multiple benchmarks, suggesting that
<br>the pre-trained vectors are ‘universal’ feature ex-
<br>tractors that can be utilized for various classiﬁca-
<br>tion tasks. Learning task-speciﬁc vectors through
<br>ﬁne-tuning results in further improvements. We
<br>ﬁnally describe a simple modiﬁcation to the archi-
<br>tecture to allow for the use of both pre-trained and
<br>task-speciﬁc vectors by having multiple channels.
<br>Our work is philosophically similar to Razavian
<br>et al. (2014) which showed that for image clas-
<br>siﬁcation, feature extractors obtained from a pre-
<br>trained deep learning model perform well on a va-
<br>riety of tasks—including tasks that are very dif-
<br>ferent from the original task for which the feature
<br>extractors were trained.

<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">and this is indeed reﬂected in the learned vectors.
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">For (randomly initialized) tokens not in the set
<br>of pre-trained vectors, ﬁne-tuning allows them to
<br>learn more meaningful representations: the net-
<br>work learns that exclamation marks are associ-
<br>ated with effusive expressions and that commas
<br>are conjunctive (table 3).
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:72px; top:4297px; width:218px; height:45px;"><span style="font-family: AFMCTD+NimbusRomNo9L-Medi; font-size:14px">4.3 Further Observations
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">We report on some further experiments and obser-
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">vations:
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:83px; top:4348px; width:100px; height:18px;"><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">• </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">Kalchbrenner et al.
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:93px; top:4353px; width:196px; height:26px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">(2014) report much
<br>worse results with a CNN that has essentially
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:329px; top:3678px; width:196px; height:107px;"><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">the same architecture as our single channel
<br>model. For example, their Max-TDNN (Time
<br>Delay Neural Network) with randomly ini-
<br>tialized words obtains </span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">37</span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">.</span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">4% </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">on the SST-1
<br>dataset, compared to </span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">45</span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">.</span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">0% </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">for our model.
<br>We attribute such discrepancy to our CNN
<br>having much more capacity (multiple ﬁlter
<br>widths and feature maps).
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:318px; top:3788px; width:206px; height:73px;"><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">• </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">Dropout proved to be such a good regularizer
<br>that it was ﬁne to use a larger than necessary
<br>network and simply let dropout regularize it.
<br>Dropout consistently added 2%–4% relative
<br>performance.
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:318px; top:3864px; width:206px; height:140px;"><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">• </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">When randomly initializing words not in
<br></span><span style="font-family: UPTTJQ+NimbusMonL-Regu; font-size:11px">word2vec</span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">, we obtained slight improve-
<br>ments by sampling each dimension from
<br></span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">U</span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">[</span><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">−</span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">a, a</span><span style="font-family: RODBTP+CMR10; font-size:13px">] </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">where </span><span style="font-family: QYDMTJ+CMMI10; font-size:10px">a </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">was chosen such that the
<br>randomly initialized vectors have the same
<br>variance as the pre-trained ones. It would be
<br>interesting to see if employing more sophis-
<br>ticated methods to mirror the distribution of
<br>pre-trained vectors in the initialization pro-
<br>cess gives further improvements.
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:318px; top:4008px; width:206px; height:100px;"><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">• </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">We brieﬂy experimented with another set of
<br>publicly available word vectors trained by
<br>Collobert et al. (2011) on Wikipedia,</span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:9px">8 </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">and
<br>found that </span><span style="font-family: UPTTJQ+NimbusMonL-Regu; font-size:11px">word2vec </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">gave far superior per-
<br>formance. It is not clear whether this is due
<br>to Mikolov et al. (2013)’s architecture or the
<br>100 billion word Google News dataset.
<br></span></div><div style="position:absolute; border: textbox 1px solid; writing-mode:lr-tb; left:307px; top:4111px; width:218px; height:184px;"><span style="font-family: ORAFIC+CMSY10; font-size:18px">• </span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">Adadelta (Zeiler, 2012) gave similar results
<br>to Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) but required
<br>fewer epochs.
<br></span><span style="font-family: AFMCTD+NimbusRomNo9L-Medi; font-size:15px">5 Conclusion
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">In the present work we have described a series of
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">experiments with convolutional neural networks
<br></span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">built on top of </span><span style="font-family: UPTTJQ+NimbusMonL-Regu; font-size:11px">word2vec</span><span style="font-family: FGFCHU+NimbusRomNo9L-Regu; font-size:13px">. Despite little tuning
<br>of hyperparameters, a simple CNN with one layer
<br>of convolution performs remarkably well. Our re-
<br>sults add to the well-established evidence that un-
<br>supervised pre-training of word vectors is an im-
<br>portant ingredient in deep learning for NLP.
